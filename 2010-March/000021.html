  My work is an epistolary travelogue (18c, collating both ms and print versions).  
It's easiest to work one letter at a time (right now, article class) and have all the 
versions before me on a big desk.  If I don't focus entirely on one letter, I'm 
likely to miss a variant.  My computer desk rolls up alongside the desk covered 
with books and papers. My edition has a standard three stack (text, variants, 
commentary), and I work on the commentary notes as I go along too.      

My procedure is like pretty much like Michael's.  Not a full collation, just a 
record of changes and emendations.  But recorded variants still take up much 
space.  The control text is a printed first edition (for spelling and other 
accidentals), into which I emend words from the author's ms.  Typed in a long 
time ago. 

Because so many variants need to be recorded, the text will end on up on the 
bottom of page, but the variants appear at the top of the next page.  Each 
update makes TeXShop/ledmac better about keeping text and notes together 
(thank you), but I add another text to the collation and ledmac gets a little 
cranky.  Me too, I suppose :  ).   None of the suggested fixes for changing page 
breaks have worked.  I get white spaces or text in the margins.   

Thank you for asking; hope this helps.  Back to work.

>On variant collecting:  I have typed in my starter text from the "best" 
manuscript and I first do a quick clean up for spelling and things that are 
obvious.  Then I go through word by word and check each manuscript, including 
rechecking the one that the starter text was based off of, and I enter the 
variants.  Luckily for this edition I am only using 3 manuscripts, so I have the 
scans of them all open at once in the top part of my screen.  I usually will do 
five or ten verses for each manuscript before switching to the next, as that 
keeps it a bit interesting, but I don't get too far ahead either.  Sometimes a 
variant in another manuscript prompt me to go back and recheck my 
transcription of a previous reading, so not going too far ahead helps make that 
quicker.  And of course with manuscripts letters occasionally look ambiguous 
and what you actually decide the manuscript reads will depend on what you 
think it should read in some instances.  For the really ambiguous letters I
>  use special brackets to indicate uncertainty.
